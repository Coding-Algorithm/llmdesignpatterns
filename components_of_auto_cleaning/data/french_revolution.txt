The French Revolution was a period of radical political and social transformation in France
that began with the Estates General of 1789 and ended with the formation of the French
Consulate in November 1799. Many of its ideas are considered fundamental principles of
liberal democracy, while the Revolution also initiated a cycle of global conflicts that
extended until 1815.

The causes of the Revolution were many and complex. France faced a severe fiscal crisis
after decades of costly wars, including the American Revolutionary War. The tax system was
deeply inequitable, placing the heaviest burdens on the peasantry while the nobility and
clergy enjoyed significant exemptions. Poor harvests in the late 1780s caused widespread
hunger and social unrest, and Enlightenment ideas about individual rights and popular
sovereignty had given educated citizens a new language with which to criticise the monarchy.

The storming of the Bastille on July 14, 1789, became the iconic moment of the Revolution.
The Bastille was a medieval fortress and prison that symbolised royal tyranny. A crowd of
Parisians, fearing a royalist military crackdown, attacked and seized it. Although only
seven prisoners were held there at the time, the fall of the Bastille electrified France
and marked the beginning of popular insurrection against the old order.

The Revolution passed through several distinct phases, moving from constitutional monarchy
to the radical democracy of the Terror, and finally to the more conservative rule of the
Directory. Each phase was marked by intense ideological conflict and, often, political
violence. The legacy of the Revolution continues to shape French politics and culture to
the present day.
