A neural network is a computational model loosely inspired by the structure of biological
neural networks in the brain. It consists of layers of interconnected nodes, or neurons,
each of which applies a simple mathematical transformation to its inputs and passes the
result to the next layer.

The fundamental building block is the perceptron, introduced by Frank Rosenblatt in 1958.
A perceptron computes a weighted sum of its inputs, adds a bias term, and passes the result
through an activation function to produce an output. When many perceptrons are arranged in
layers and trained together, they can learn to approximate remarkably complex functions.

Training a neural network involves two phases. In the forward pass, input data flows through
the network layer by layer to produce a prediction. In the backward pass, the error of that
prediction is propagated back through the network using the chain rule of calculus, and each
weight is nudged slightly in the direction that reduces the error. Repeat this process
millions of times across a large dataset, and the network gradually learns to make accurate
predictions.

Modern deep learning architectures extend this basic idea with innovations such as
convolutional layers for spatial data, recurrent connections for sequential data, and
attention mechanisms that allow the model to dynamically weight the relevance of different
parts of its input. These advances have driven breakthroughs in image recognition, natural
language processing, and scientific discovery.
